July 2026

On covering GBNAF's Dignity & Shelter outreach

On waiting, color, and showing up consistently enough that the right moment eventually turns toward you — notes from covering GBNAF's Dignity & Shelter outreach in Abuja.

+

I've been documenting community outreach for a while now through my work with The Humanity Anchor Initiative, and every time I think I've adjusted to what these camps look like, something resets that. This one was the GBNAF Dignity & Shelter Basket program, clothing and household essentials distributed to residents of an IDP camp here in Abuja. What struck me shooting it wasn't the scale, though the crowd was large. It was the waiting. The color of it, actually. Rows of hijabs in blue, green, orange, pink, all standing in dust-colored ground under a tin roof, patient in a way that felt heavier than the word "patient" usually carries. I didn't direct anything. Everyone in these frames is doing exactly what they were doing before I lifted the camera: queuing, minding a child, talking to a coordinator, waiting for their name or their turn. My job was just to keep up and not get in the way. There's a shot in this set of a coordinator's back, "GBNAF COORDINATOR" printed across his shirt, facing a mother holding her child. I didn't plan that frame. He just turned at the right moment. That's most of what documentary work actually is: showing up consistently enough that the right moment eventually turns toward you.
